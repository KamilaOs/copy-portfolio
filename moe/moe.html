Why you don't crave sex out of nowhere — and why that's completely normal

You love your partner. You enjoy sex when it happens. So why don't you think about it more often? The answer is simpler than you think: nothing is wrong.

Meet Sam
A story that might sound familiar

Sam spent years feeling broken. Sam loves their partner and enjoys intimacy, but never feels that sudden "I need you right now" urge. No daydreams about sex. No spontaneous cravings.

Then Sam learned about the two types of sexual desire. Once Sam stopped waiting to feel like initiating and started staying open to connection — everything changed.

If Sam's story sounds familiar, keep reading. Because what most people are taught about desire is only half the picture.

What is desire?

Desire is the mental and emotional pull that makes you think about, seek out, or feel open to sexual experiences. It is different from arousal — which is the physical response in your body.

Desire lives on a spectrum and changes with stress, hormones, health, relationship dynamics, and your cycle. Sometimes it is about physical craving. Other times it is about wanting closeness, or simply feeling wanted.

Your desire is not broken. It might just work differently than you've been told.
The two types of desire

Research by Dr. Rosemary Basson identified two main patterns of sexual desire. Knowing yours can transform how you approach intimacy.

⚡
Type 01

Spontaneous

Appears on its own, without any trigger. You might wake up turned on or suddenly think about your partner during the day.

Desire → Seek experience
🌿
Type 02

Responsive

Builds in response to something: a gentle touch, emotional connection, or a relaxed moment. It doesn't arrive uninvited — it grows once intimacy has started.

Experience begins → Desire builds

Many people — especially in long-term relationships — experience responsive desire more often. If this sounds like you, it does not mean your libido is low. It simply means your desire needs a spark.

Common misconceptions
Myth
If you don't feel randomly turned on, you have low libido.
Truth
Responsive desire is not the same as low desire. If you enjoy sex once it starts and feel satisfied afterward, your desire is working normally.
Myth
Responsive desire is a "female trait."
Truth
It is reported more often by people assigned female at birth, but it is not a strict gender rule. People of all genders experience responsive desire.
Myth
Your desire type is fixed for life.
Truth
Most people shift between types depending on hormones, stress, health, relationship stage, and medication. It changes — and that's normal.
Working with your desire type

If you have responsive desire

  • Start with non-sexual touch that feels good — cuddling, gentle massage. Let arousal build naturally.
  • Identify what turns you on and what turns you off.
  • Schedule time for connection. It's practical, not unromantic.
  • Communicate openly about what feels good.

If you have spontaneous desire

  • Don't skip the emotional connection. Daily affection makes intimacy richer.
  • Build anticipation with small gestures throughout the day.
  • Learn your partner's preferences through conversation.
  • Remember — your own needs matter too. You deserve to feel pursued as well.

When partners have different desire types, it is common and workable. One often does more initiating while the other stays open to connection. Neither approach is wrong.

When desire shifts

Desire naturally changes during different life stages. During your cycle, many notice more spontaneous desire around ovulation and responsive or lower interest at other times. Tracking in Moe helps you see your personal patterns.

Starting or stopping hormonal contraception, having children, entering long-term relationships, menopause — all of these can shift desire. This is usually temporary, and always valid.

🩺

When to speak to a doctor

Fluctuations are normal. Reach out to a healthcare provider if desire changes cause you distress, happen suddenly, come with pain during sex, or appear alongside mood shifts, fatigue, or irregular periods.

Your desire is valid.

Whether your desire is spontaneous, responsive, higher, or lower — you are within the normal range of human sexuality. Many who thought they had low libido simply discovered they had responsive desire. They still genuinely want intimacy. Just in a different way.

Understanding your desire type helps you learn how your body and mind actually work. Above all, you deserve a sex life that feels good — one that honours your body, needs, boundaries, and pleasure.
