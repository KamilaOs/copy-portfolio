Where Does Women's Love for True Crime Actually Come From?

Turns out, psychology has a lot to say about it.

1 AM. Three episodes deep. Still can't turn it off.

You know the type. Maybe you are the type.

It's 1 AM. You're three episodes deep into a documentary about a serial killer from the 1980s, and somehow it still feels impossible to turn off.

Your boyfriend is confused.

Honestly, maybe you are too.

So why do so many women find themselves completely hooked on true crime? Turns out, psychology has a lot to say about it.

We're wired to ask "why?"

Most of us have a hard time wrapping our heads around how someone could hurt another person. We feel guilty swatting a fly.

So when we hear about someone capable of real, sustained cruelty, something in our brain needs to make sense of it. Watching true crime is how a lot of us try to do that. We analyze, we empathize, we try to understand what the rest of us simply don't feel.

It hits closer to home than we'd like to admit
Nearly one in three women worldwide has experienced physical or sexual violence in her lifetime.

And that's not even counting the near-misses. The moments that almost became something worse.

True crime can feel, oddly, like therapy. A reminder that you're not alone. That what happened, or almost happened, wasn't your fault, and wasn't unique to you.

Justice feels good. Really good.

We grow up on stories where the villain loses, and for good reason. It teaches us that doing harm has consequences. True crime delivers that same satisfaction, but it's real. The bad guy actually gets caught. Watching justice play out on screen scratches an itch that feels deeply human.

It's a little bit rebellious, too

A lot of us grew up being told to be small. Be gentle. Like the right things.

And even as we got older and pushed back, insisting we could carry the chairs, lift the boxes, handle ourselves, there was always that quiet pressure to stay in a certain lane.

True crime is a refusal to do that. It's dark, it's uncomfortable, it doesn't apologize for itself. Choosing to engage with something that makes people go "how can you watch that?" is its own small act of not shrinking.

It's also genuinely useful

Nobody sat us down and taught us to spot the warning signs. We got vague advice. Don't go out alone. Don't trust strangers. Nothing that prepared us for the complicated, subtle, broad-daylight ways danger actually shows up.

True crime fills that gap. For a lot of women, it already has.

And sometimes? It's about control.
You can pause it.
Rewind it.
Google the ending before you even finish watching.

In a genre full of chaos and suffering, you hold all the power. For anyone who's spent their life feeling like they had none, that's not nothing.
